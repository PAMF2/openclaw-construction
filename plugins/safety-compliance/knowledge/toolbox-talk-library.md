## Talk 1: Fall Protection

**Duration:** 8 minutes
**Applicable Standard:** 29 CFR 1926 Subpart M (1926.500-503)
**Target Audience:** All trades working at heights

### Opening Statement

Falls are the number one killer in construction — accounting for roughly one-third of all construction deaths every year. Today we're going to talk about how to protect ourselves from falls.

### Key Points

**1. The 6-Foot Rule**
In construction, fall protection is required whenever you're working at a height of 6 feet or more above a lower level. This includes working near unprotected edges, holes, roof edges, elevated platforms, and scaffold platforms.

**2. Three Types of Fall Protection**
- **Guardrails**: The preferred method — a passive system that doesn't require worker action. Top rail at 42 inches, mid rail, and toeboard.
- **Personal Fall Arrest System (PFAS)**: Full body harness + lanyard + anchor point. Used when guardrails aren't feasible.
- **Safety Nets**: Installed below work area to catch falling workers. Less common on most jobs.

**3. Inspecting Your PFAS**
Before every use, inspect:
- **Harness**: Check all stitching, webbing for cuts/burns/fraying, D-ring condition, buckles working
- **Lanyard**: No cuts, abrasions, or knots; shock absorber intact; snap hooks locking properly
- **Anchor point**: Rated for 5,000 pounds per person; connected to structural steel, not conduit or vent pipes

**4. Know Your Fall Distance**
A 6-foot lanyard with a shock absorber can extend up to 3.5 feet during arrest, plus your height and harness stretch. Total fall clearance needed: approximately 18.5 feet below the anchor point. If you're less than 18.5 feet above the lower level with a standard lanyard, you need a shorter lanyard or an SRL (self-retracting lifeline).

**5. Holes and Covers**
All floor holes and openings must be covered or guarded. Covers must be marked "HOLE" or "COVER," secured against displacement, and capable of supporting 2x the maximum intended load.

**6. After a Fall**
If your PFAS arrests a fall, the harness, lanyard, and all components must be removed from service immediately and NOT reused. Report the incident.

### Discussion Questions

1. What are the anchor points available on our current work areas? Are they rated properly?
2. When was the last time you inspected your harness? Can everyone show me how to do it?
3. What would you do if you saw a hole cover removed or displaced?
4. Does anyone have a scenario where they weren't sure if fall protection was needed?

## Talk 2: Electrical Safety

**Duration:** 7 minutes
**Applicable Standard:** 29 CFR 1926 Subpart K (1926.400-449)
**Target Audience:** All trades

### Opening Statement

Electrocution is one of construction's Fatal Four hazards. You don't have to be an electrician to be killed by electricity — anyone using power tools, extension cords, or working near power lines is at risk.

### Key Points

**1. GFCI Protection**
Every outlet on a construction site must have GFCI (Ground Fault Circuit Interrupter) protection. Test your GFCI before each use — push the TEST button, then RESET. If it doesn't trip, do not use that outlet. Report it.

**2. Extension Cord Safety**
- Use only cords rated for hard or extra-hard usage (marked "S," "ST," "SO," or "STO")
- Three-wire (grounded) cords only — if the ground prong is missing, the cord is out of service
- No spliced cords — if the insulation is cut or the cord is repaired with tape, remove it from service
- Don't run cords through doors, windows, or holes where they'll be pinched or damaged
- Don't daisy-chain cords (plug one into another) excessively

**3. Lockout/Tagout**
Before working on any electrical system, circuit, or equipment:
- De-energize the circuit
- Lock the breaker/disconnect in the OFF position with YOUR lock
- Tag it with YOUR name, date, and reason
- Test to verify it's de-energized
- Never remove someone else's lock

**4. Power Line Awareness**
- Stay at least 20 feet from overhead power lines at all times (people, equipment, materials)
- If a crane, ladder, scaffold, or material can reach within 20 feet of a power line, STOP and get a plan
- If equipment contacts a power line: stay in the cab, call for help, warn others to stay away. Only jump clear (land with feet together) if there is a fire

**5. Wet Conditions**
- Never use electrical equipment in standing water unless it's specifically rated for wet conditions
- Keep electrical panels, disconnects, and temporary power dry
- Use GFCI protection — it's even more critical in wet conditions

### Discussion Questions

1. Where are the GFCI outlets on this jobsite? Has anyone tested them today?
2. Has anyone noticed damaged extension cords on site? What should you do?
3. What overhead power lines are near our work areas?
4. If you found an energized panel open and unattended, what would you do?

### Sign-Off

I have attended this toolbox talk on Electrical Safety. I understand the electrical hazards on this jobsite and the required precautions.

## Talk 3: Excavation and Trenching Safety

**Duration:** 8 minutes
**Applicable Standard:** 29 CFR 1926 Subpart P (1926.650-652)
**Target Audience:** Earthwork crews, utility installers, all trades working near excavations

### Opening Statement

A cubic yard of soil weighs approximately 3,000 pounds — about the weight of a small car. When a trench wall collapses, the soil buries a worker in seconds. There is very little time for rescue, and cave-in fatalities happen every year on construction sites.

### Key Points

**1. The 5-Foot Rule**
Cave-in protection is required in all excavations 5 feet deep or more, unless the excavation is made entirely in stable rock. Four options: sloping, benching, shoring, or shielding (trench box).

**2. Competent Person**
A competent person must inspect excavations daily before work begins, after every rainstorm, and after any event that could affect stability (vibration, adjacent loads, etc.). The competent person classifies the soil and selects protective measures.

**3. Access and Egress**
When a trench is 4 feet deep or more, you must have a ladder, ramp, or stairway within 25 feet of every worker. That means no one should ever have to walk more than 25 feet to reach a way out.

**4. Utility Locates**
Call 811 at least 48-72 hours before any excavation. Hand-dig within the tolerance zone (typically 18-24 inches on each side of the marked utility). Hitting a gas line or electrical line in a trench can be fatal.

**5. Spoil Pile**
Excavated material (spoil) must be placed at least 2 feet back from the edge of the trench. Tools, equipment, and other materials also must stay 2 feet back. Nothing should be stored at the edge where it can fall in or add load to the trench wall.

**6. Water**
Water in a trench is extremely dangerous — it weakens soil and changes soil classification. Never enter a trench with standing water unless special precautions are in place (dewatering, upgraded protective system). A competent person must evaluate the situation.

**7. Warning Signs**
Watch for: tension cracks in the ground surface, bulging at the base of the trench wall, water seeping from walls, previously backfilled ground, vibration from nearby equipment or traffic, and changes in soil color or texture within the trench.

### Discussion Questions

1. Who is the competent person for excavations on this project? Does everyone know who that is?
2. Where are the ladders positioned in our current excavations? Are they within 25 feet?
3. What would you do if you noticed cracks in the ground near a trench edge?
4. What is the soil classification for our current excavation work?

### Sign-Off

## Talk 4: Heat Illness Prevention

**Duration:** 8 minutes
**Applicable Standard:** OSHA General Duty Clause (Section 5(a)(1)); Cal-OSHA Title 8, Section 3395
**Target Audience:** All outdoor workers, especially during warm months

### Opening Statement

Heat kills more construction workers than any other weather-related hazard. Heat illness can progress from heat cramps to heat exhaustion to heat stroke in minutes — and heat stroke can be fatal. The good news is that heat illness is 100% preventable if we follow the right procedures.

### Key Points

**1. Water**
Drink water before you're thirsty. By the time you feel thirsty, you're already dehydrating. The rule is: one quart (32 oz) per hour in hot conditions. That's about one cup every 15 minutes. Water must be cool, clean, and easily accessible — within walking distance at all times. Sports drinks are acceptable but not a substitute for water.

**2. Rest**
Take rest breaks in the shade. When the heat index is above 95 degrees F, we require a minimum 10-minute cool-down rest every 2 hours — more frequently in extreme heat. Don't wait until you feel bad. Scheduled breaks prevent problems.

**3. Shade**
Shade must be available whenever the temperature exceeds 80 degrees F. Shade structures, canopies, trees, or the shadow side of a building all count. Shade must be close enough to use during breaks — not across the site.

**4. Acclimatization**
New workers and workers returning from more than a week off are at the highest risk. It takes 7-14 days for your body to adjust to working in heat. During the first week, new or returning workers should work shorter shifts, take more breaks, and be closely monitored. Over 50% of heat fatalities occur in the first week on the job.

**5. Know the Signs**

| Condition | Symptoms | Action |
|-----------|----------|--------|
| Heat Cramps | Muscle cramps, heavy sweating | Move to shade, drink water, stretch |
| Heat Exhaustion | Headache, dizziness, nausea, heavy sweating, clammy skin, weakness, fast pulse | Move to shade, remove excess clothing, cool with water, drink water, get medical help if no improvement in 15 min |
| Heat Stroke | High body temp (>103F), hot/red/dry skin, confusion, slurred speech, loss of consciousness | CALL 911 IMMEDIATELY. Cool worker aggressively (ice, cold water). This is a life-threatening emergency. |

**6. Buddy System**
Work in pairs. Watch each other for signs of heat illness. If your coworker seems confused, dizzy, or is acting strangely — stop them, get them in the shade, and call for help. Don't assume they're "just tired."

### Discussion Questions

1. Where are the water coolers located on site today? Is there enough for everyone?
2. Where is the closest shade structure to your work area?
3. Is anyone new to the jobsite or returning from time off? (Acclimatization reminder)
4. What's the emergency plan if someone has heat stroke? Who calls 911? Where do we meet the ambulance?

### Sign-Off

I have attended this toolbox talk on Heat Illness Prevention. I understand the importance of water, rest, and shade, and I know how to recognize and respond to heat illness.

## Talk 5: Scaffold Safety

**Duration:** 8 minutes
**Applicable Standard:** 29 CFR 1926 Subpart L (1926.450-454)
**Target Audience:** All trades using scaffolds

### Opening Statement

Scaffolding injuries are among the most common in construction. Falls from scaffolds, objects falling from scaffolds, and scaffold collapses are all preventable. Whether you're erecting scaffold, working on it, or working below it, you need to know these rules.

### Key Points

**1. Only Trained Workers**
You must be trained by a qualified person to work on scaffolds. Training covers hazard recognition, fall protection, load limits, and proper use. If you haven't been trained, do not go on a scaffold.

**2. Competent Person Inspections**
A competent person must inspect every scaffold before each work shift and after any event that could affect the scaffold (wind storm, impact, modification). If the scaffold tag says "DO NOT USE" — do not use it.

**3. Guardrails**
All scaffold platforms 10 feet or more above a lower level must have guardrails on all open sides and ends: top rail at 38-45 inches, mid rail, and toeboard. If guardrails are missing, do not work on that platform.

**4. Platforms**
- Platforms must be fully planked — no gaps wider than 1 inch between planks or at the edge
- Platforms must be at least 18 inches wide
- Scaffold-grade planks only — no random lumber
- Planks must extend at least 6 inches past the frame, but not more than 12 inches (unless cleated)

**5. Access**
Use the designated ladder, stair tower, or built-in access to reach scaffold platforms. Never climb on cross-braces — this is one of the most common scaffold violations. Cross-braces are not designed to be climbed on and can fail under your weight.

**6. Load Limits**
Know the rated capacity of your scaffold. Light duty = 25 PSF. Medium duty = 50 PSF. Heavy duty = 75 PSF. Don't overload platforms with materials. Don't stack materials higher than your guardrails. If multiple trades are sharing a scaffold, coordinate loading.

**7. Base and Foundation**
Scaffolds must be on a firm, level surface. Base plates and mudsills required. Never use unstable objects (concrete blocks, barrels, boxes) to support scaffolds. Wheels on mobile scaffolds must be locked when workers are on the platform.

### Discussion Questions

1. Who is the competent person for scaffolds on this project?
2. Is the scaffold you're using today fully planked with guardrails? When was it last inspected?
3. What is the rated capacity of the scaffolds we're using?
4. Can you show me the proper access point for the scaffold?

## Talk 6: Crane Operations and Rigging Safety

**Duration:** 10 minutes
**Applicable Standard:** 29 CFR 1926 Subpart N (1926.1400-1442)
**Target Audience:** Crane operators, riggers, signal persons, all workers near crane operations

### Opening Statement

Cranes move the heaviest loads on any construction site. When something goes wrong — an overload, a rigging failure, contact with a power line — the consequences are catastrophic. Everyone on site needs to understand the basics of working safely around cranes.

### Key Points

**1. Qualified Personnel Only**
- **Operator**: Must be certified (NCCCO or equivalent) and evaluated by the employer
- **Signal person**: Must be qualified — knows standard hand signals and radio communication
- **Rigger**: Must be qualified — understands load weights, rigging hardware, and hitch configurations

**2. Never Under the Load**
No one walks or stands under a suspended load. Period. If a crane is lifting material over an area, that area must be barricaded and cleared of people. The swing radius of the crane counterweight must also be barricaded.

**3. Power Lines — 20 Feet**
Maintain a minimum 20-foot clearance from all power lines for the crane, load, rigging, and tag lines. If work must occur within 20 feet, the power line must be de-energized and grounded, or a detailed plan must be developed with the utility company. Electrocution from power line contact is the leading cause of crane fatalities.

**4. Load Chart**
Every crane has a load chart that tells you exactly how much it can lift at a given radius and boom length. The operator must follow the load chart for every lift. Exceeding the rated capacity is the leading cause of crane tip-overs.

**5. Rigging Inspection**
Before every lift, inspect:
- Slings: No cuts, kinks, crushing, birdcaging (wire rope); no burns, cuts, melting (synthetic)
- Shackles: Correct size and rated capacity; pin fully threaded and tight
- Hooks: Safety latch working; no cracks, bending, or throat opening
- All rigging hardware: Legible capacity tags; proper configuration for the load

**6. Tag Lines**
Use tag lines to control the load and prevent spinning. Tag lines must be non-conductive near power lines. Only qualified riggers should attach and manage tag lines.

**7. Communication**
Clear communication between the operator and signal person is essential. Use standard ASME hand signals or clear radio communication. Only one person gives signals to the operator (designated signal person). Anyone can give a STOP signal.

**8. Wind**
Suspend crane operations when wind exceeds the manufacturer's rating (typically 20-30 mph for mobile cranes). High winds dramatically reduce crane capacity and make loads difficult to control.

### Discussion Questions

1. Who is the certified crane operator on site? Who is the designated signal person?
2. Where are the power lines in relation to today's crane work?
3. Can you identify the standard hand signal for "STOP"? (Everyone should know this one.)
4. What is the emergency plan if a crane contacts a power line?

### Sign-Off

I have attended this toolbox talk on Crane Operations and Rigging Safety. I understand the hazards of crane operations and the required safety procedures.

## Talk 7: Personal Protective Equipment (PPE)

**Duration:** 6 minutes
**Applicable Standard:** 29 CFR 1926 Subpart E (1926.95-107)
**Target Audience:** All construction workers

### Opening Statement

PPE is your last line of defense. We use engineering controls and safe work practices first, but when those aren't enough, PPE protects you. It only works if you wear it correctly and maintain it properly.

### Key Points

**1. Hard Hat**
Required at all times on most construction sites. Must be ANSI Z89.1 compliant. Inspect daily — look for cracks, dents, or penetrations. Replace after any impact. Do not drill holes, paint, or modify. Replace per manufacturer guidelines (typically every 5 years for the shell, 1 year for the suspension). Wear it the right way — brim forward unless the hard hat is specifically rated for reverse wear.

**2. Eye Protection**
Safety glasses (ANSI Z87.1) are the minimum on most sites. Upgrade to goggles for grinding, chipping, cutting, or chemical splash risk. Face shield over safety glasses for heavy grinding, cutting, or chemical handling. Prescription safety glasses must also meet Z87.1.

**3. Hearing Protection**
Required when noise exceeds 85 dBA (action level). If you have to shout to be heard 3 feet away, you probably need hearing protection. Options: foam ear plugs (NRR 29-33), ear muffs (NRR 21-31), or both for high-noise environments. Insert foam plugs correctly: roll tight, pull ear up and back, insert, hold until expanded.

**4. High-Visibility Clothing**
Required near vehicle and equipment traffic (ANSI 107 Class 2 or 3). Vest or high-vis shirt. Keep it clean — a dirty vest doesn't reflect. Replace if faded or damaged.

**5. Hand Protection**
Gloves matched to the hazard: leather for general construction, cut-resistant for sharp materials, chemical-resistant for chemicals, insulated for electrical work. Do not wear gloves near rotating equipment (drill press, lathe) — they can get caught.

**6. Foot Protection**
Steel-toe or composite-toe safety boots (ASTM F2413). Required on all construction sites. Must cover the ankle. Metatarsal guards for heavy material handling. Keep boots in good condition — worn soles mean slip hazard.

**7. Respiratory Protection**
When dust, fumes, or chemical vapors are present and engineering controls are insufficient. Dust mask (N95) for nuisance dust. Half-face respirator with appropriate cartridges for silica, painting, chemical exposure. Must be fit-tested and medically cleared before use.

### Discussion Questions

1. Is everyone wearing the required PPE right now? (Visual inspection)
2. When was your hard hat last inspected? How old is it?
3. What specific PPE hazards exist in your work today that go beyond the standard site PPE?
4. Has everyone using respirators been fit-tested this year?

## Talk 8: Confined Space Entry

**Duration:** 10 minutes
**Applicable Standard:** 29 CFR 1926 Subpart AA (1926.1200-1213)
**Target Audience:** Workers entering confined spaces (manholes, tanks, vaults, pits)

### Opening Statement

A confined space is not designed for continuous occupancy, has limited means of entry/exit, and is large enough for a worker to enter and perform work. Examples on a construction site include manholes, utility vaults, tanks, pits, and some excavations. Confined spaces can contain hazardous atmospheres that will kill you in minutes — or seconds.

### Key Points

**1. Permit-Required Confined Space (PRCS)**
A confined space becomes "permit-required" when it contains or has the potential for: hazardous atmosphere, engulfment hazard, converging walls, or any other recognized serious safety/health hazard. Most construction confined spaces are permit-required.

**2. Atmospheric Hazards**
- **Oxygen deficiency** (< 19.5%) — causes disorientation, unconsciousness, death
- **Oxygen enrichment** (> 23.5%) — increases fire/explosion risk
- **Flammable gas/vapor** (> 10% LEL) — explosion risk
- **Toxic gases** (H2S, CO, etc.) — H2S at 100 ppm = immediately dangerous; CO at 1200 ppm = immediately dangerous

**3. Testing Before Entry**
Test the atmosphere BEFORE anyone enters, in this order:
1. Oxygen (19.5% - 23.5% acceptable range)
2. Flammable gases (< 10% of LEL)
3. Toxic gases (below PEL)
Test from outside the space using a remote probe. Test at multiple levels (top, middle, bottom). Continuously monitor while workers are inside.

**4. Ventilation**
Ventilate the space with a blower and duct before and during entry. Fresh air ventilation can eliminate many atmospheric hazards, but it must run continuously. Never ventilate with pure oxygen — it creates extreme fire risk.

**5. Entry Permit**
A written permit is required for every PRCS entry. The permit lists:
- Location and description of space
- Purpose of entry
- Authorized entrants, attendants, entry supervisor
- Hazards identified
- Controls in place
- Atmospheric monitoring results
- Rescue provisions
- Duration of entry
The entry supervisor signs the permit before entry begins.

**6. Attendant**
At least one attendant must remain outside the confined space at all times while workers are inside. The attendant:
- Monitors conditions
- Maintains communication with entrants
- Keeps unauthorized people out
- Calls for rescue if needed
- NEVER enters the space to attempt rescue (this is the #1 cause of multiple fatalities in confined spaces)

**7. Rescue Plan**
A rescue plan must be in place BEFORE entry begins. Options: on-site rescue team (trained and equipped), off-site rescue service (fire department with confirmed capability and response time), or self-rescue (retrieval system with tripod and winch). Non-entry rescue (retrieval line) is preferred when feasible.

### Discussion Questions

1. What confined spaces exist on this project? Have they been identified and labeled?
2. Who is trained for confined space entry? Attendant duty? Entry supervisor?
3. What is our rescue plan? What is the response time for our rescue service?
4. Where are the gas monitors? Have they been calibrated/bump-tested today?

## Talk 9: Silica Dust Exposure

**Duration:** 7 minutes
**Applicable Standard:** 29 CFR 1926.1153 — Respirable Crystalline Silica
**Target Audience:** Concrete/masonry cutters, grinders, drillers, demolition workers

### Opening Statement

Silica dust is generated every time you cut, grind, drill, or saw concrete, masonry, stone, or mortar. The dust particles are so small you can't see them, and they cause permanent lung damage. Silicosis is an incurable disease that can be fatal. The good news: following simple controls eliminates the hazard.

### Key Points

**1. What Produces Silica Dust**
- Cutting concrete or masonry with a saw
- Grinding concrete surfaces
- Core drilling through concrete or block
- Tuckpointing mortar joints
- Jack-hammering concrete
- Mixing concrete, mortar, or grout
- Sweeping dry concrete dust (never dry-sweep — always wet or HEPA vacuum)

**2. OSHA Table 1 Controls**
OSHA provides Table 1 — a list of common tasks with the specific engineering controls and respiratory protection required for each. If you follow Table 1, you're in compliance. No air monitoring needed.

For example:
- **Cutting with a handheld saw**: Use integrated water delivery system to suppress dust. Wear an N95 respirator.
- **Grinding with a handheld grinder**: Use a shroud connected to a HEPA vacuum. Wear an N95 respirator.
- **Core drilling**: Use integrated water delivery or a HEPA vacuum dust collection system.

**3. Water Is Your Best Friend**
Wet cutting and wet drilling are the most effective ways to control silica dust. Keep the water flowing — a dry cut for even a few seconds generates a hazardous dust cloud. Make sure your saw or drill has a functioning water attachment and that it stays connected.

**4. HEPA Vacuum**
When water isn't practical, use a HEPA-filtered vacuum dust collection system attached to your tool. The vacuum must be a true HEPA filter (99.97% efficiency at 0.3 microns). Regular shop vacs DO NOT work for silica.

**5. Respiratory Protection**
Even with engineering controls, a respirator is usually required per Table 1. The minimum is an N95 filtering facepiece respirator (dust mask). For higher exposures (tuckpointing, indoor grinding), a half-face respirator with P100 filters or better is required. You must be fit-tested and medically cleared to wear a respirator.

**6. Housekeeping**
NEVER dry sweep silica dust. Use a HEPA vacuum or wet sweeping methods. Compressed air for cleaning is prohibited unless no other method is feasible and respiratory protection is used. Clean up dust accumulation regularly — don't let it build up.

**7. Medical Surveillance**
If you're required to wear a respirator for silica for 30 or more days per year, you're entitled to medical surveillance including chest X-ray and lung function testing every 3 years (annually if exposure triggers are met).

### Discussion Questions

1. What silica-generating tasks are happening on site today?
2. Is the water delivery system working on your concrete saw?
3. When was your respirator last fit-tested?
4. How are we cleaning up concrete dust at the end of the day?

## Talk 10: Housekeeping and Slip/Trip/Fall Prevention

**Duration:** 6 minutes
**Applicable Standard:** 29 CFR 1926.25 (Housekeeping), 1926.1050-1060 (Stairways/Ladders)
**Target Audience:** All construction workers

### Opening Statement

Slips, trips, and falls on the same level — not falls from height, but simply tripping over debris or slipping on a wet surface — are the most common injury on construction sites. They cause sprains, fractures, and back injuries that keep people out of work for weeks. A clean, organized worksite is a safe worksite.

### Key Points

**1. Clean As You Go**
Don't wait until the end of the day to clean up. Remove scrap, cut-offs, packaging, and trash as you generate it. Stack materials neatly. Coil cords and hoses when not in use. Every trade is responsible for cleaning up after their own work.

**2. Walking Paths Must Be Clear**
Hallways, stairways, doorways, and primary walking paths must remain clear at all times. Do not store materials, tools, or debris in walkways. If you have to walk around something, it's in the wrong place.

**3. Cords and Hoses**
Extension cords, welding leads, and air hoses are major tripping hazards. Route them out of walkways. If they must cross a walking path, use cord covers or elevate them. Don't let them hang across doorways or stairways where someone will clothesline themselves.

**4. Nails and Sharp Objects**
Pull or bend over all protruding nails immediately. Stack lumber neatly — no loose boards with nails sticking up. Collect and dispose of sharp objects (razor blades, wire ends, metal shavings) promptly.

**5. Wet and Slippery Surfaces**
Mop up spills immediately — water, oil, mud, paint. Use "WET FLOOR" signs when a surface is being cleaned or when a spill can't be immediately addressed. In winter, treat icy surfaces with salt or sand before they become walkways. Sawcut slurry and concrete washout water create extremely slippery conditions.

**6. Stairways**
Use handrails. Handrails exist for a reason — use them every time, even if it means carrying material in one hand. Stairways must have proper lighting. Never use a stairway as a storage area.

**7. Waste Containers**
Dumpsters and trash containers must be available and accessible. When dumpsters are full, they need to be pulled and replaced — don't let trash pile up because the dumpster is overflowing. Separate recyclables where required.

**8. End of Day**
At the end of each shift:
- Tools stored, not left on walking surfaces or overhead
- Materials stacked and secured (especially sheet goods that can blow over)
- Penetrations and holes covered and marked
- Trash and debris removed or contained
- Cords and hoses coiled and stored

### Discussion Questions

1. What is the housekeeping condition of your work area right now? Can we do better?
2. Are there any tripping hazards you've noticed that haven't been addressed?
3. Is anyone leaving materials in walkways at the end of the shift?
4. Where are the waste containers closest to your work area? Are they accessible?

## Toolbox Talk Delivery Tips

1. **Keep it short** — 5-10 minutes maximum. Workers disengage after that.
2. **Be specific to today** — relate the topic to actual conditions on site today. "Yesterday I saw..." or "Today we're going to be working near..."
3. **Ask questions** — don't just lecture. Get workers talking. Their observations improve safety.
4. **Use visuals** — show the actual PPE, the actual anchor point, the actual confined space.
5. **Document attendance** — sign-in sheet is a legal document. Names must be legible. Keep records for the duration of the project plus per your company's retention policy (typically 5+ years).
6. **Vary the topics** — rotate through hazards relevant to the current phase of work. Don't repeat the same talk weekly.
7. **Follow up** — if a worker raises a concern during the talk, address it. Nothing kills credibility faster than "good question" with no follow-up action.
8. **Time it right** — deliver before work starts, not during lunch or at the end of the day when workers are distracted.
