# Toolbox Talk Library
> 10 complete, ready-to-deliver toolbox talks for construction sites. Each talk is 5-10 minutes with key points, discussion questions, and sign-off.

## Talk 1: Fall Protection

**Duration:** 8 minutes | **OSHA Standard:** 29 CFR 1926 Subpart M

### Key Points
- Falls are the #1 cause of death in construction (33% of all fatalities)
- Fall protection required at 6 feet in construction (not 10 like general industry)
- Three methods: guardrails, safety nets, personal fall arrest systems (PFAS)
- 100% tie-off when working at height — no exceptions

### PFAS Requirements
- Full body harness (not a body belt)
- Lanyard with deceleration device or self-retracting lifeline (SRL)
- Anchor point rated for 5,000 lbs per person
- Free-fall distance limited to 6 feet
- Inspect all equipment before each use — look for fraying, cracks, burns, corrosion

### Common Violations
- Unprotected edges and openings
- Missing hole covers (or covers not marked "HOLE" and not secured)
- Improper anchor points (attached to conduit, ductwork, or unsecured items)
- Using damaged equipment

### Discussion Questions
1. What fall hazards exist on our job today?
2. Has anyone seen an improperly secured hole cover this week?
3. When was the last time you inspected your harness?

## Talk 2: Electrical Safety & Lockout/Tagout

**Duration:** 7 minutes | **OSHA Standard:** 29 CFR 1926 Subpart K

### Key Points
- Electrocution is the 2nd leading cause of death in construction
- Assume all wires are energized until verified de-energized
- Maintain 10-foot clearance from overhead power lines (up to 50kV)
- GFCI protection required on all 120V, 15 and 20 amp receptacles on construction sites
- Lockout/tagout before working on any electrical system

### LOTO Procedure
1. Notify affected personnel
2. Shut down equipment using normal procedure
3. Isolate energy source (disconnect, close valve)
4. Apply lock and tag — YOUR lock, YOUR key
5. Verify zero energy (test with voltmeter)
6. Perform work
7. Remove lock/tag only when work complete and area clear

### Discussion Questions
1. Where are the panel locations on this job?
2. Do you have your personal lock with you today?
3. What would you do if you found exposed wiring?

## Talk 3: Excavation & Trenching Safety

**Duration:** 8 minutes | **OSHA Standard:** 29 CFR 1926 Subpart P

### Key Points
- Trenching is one of the most hazardous construction operations
- Cave-ins can kill in seconds — 1 cubic yard of soil weighs ~3,000 lbs
- Protective systems required for trenches 5 feet deep or more
- Competent person must inspect trenches daily and after rain/vibration events
- Call 811 at least 48 hours before digging (utility locate)

### Protective Systems
- **Sloping**: Cut walls back at safe angle (varies by soil type)
  - Type A soil: 3/4:1 (53 degree angle)
  - Type B soil: 1:1 (45 degree angle)
  - Type C soil: 1.5:1 (34 degree angle)
- **Shoring**: Hydraulic or timber supports against trench walls
- **Shielding**: Trench boxes/shields placed in the trench

### Rules
- Spoil pile must be at least 2 feet from trench edge
- Ladder access within 25 feet of all workers in trenches 4+ feet deep
- No work under raised loads (backhoe bucket)
- Water accumulation must be controlled before entry

### Discussion Questions
1. What type of soil are we working in today?
2. Where is the nearest ladder access in our trench?
3. Have utilities been located and marked?

## Talk 4: Heat Illness Prevention

**Duration:** 7 minutes | **OSHA Standard:** General Duty Clause + state-specific

### Key Points
- Heat kills more construction workers than any other weather hazard
- Heat illness progresses: cramps → exhaustion → stroke (medical emergency)
- Risk factors: high temp, humidity, direct sun, physical exertion, lack of acclimatization
- New workers are at HIGHEST risk — 50% of heat deaths occur in first week on job

### Prevention (Water-Rest-Shade)
- **Water**: Drink before you're thirsty. 1 cup every 15-20 minutes. NOT energy drinks.
- **Rest**: 10-minute break in shade every 2 hours (more frequent in extreme heat)
- **Shade**: Must be available. If no natural shade, provide canopy/tent.
- **Acclimatization**: New workers — 20% workload Day 1, increase 20% per day over 5 days

### Heat Index Action Levels
| Heat Index | Risk Level | Action |
|-----------|------------|--------|
| < 80°F | Low | Standard precautions |
| 80-90°F | Moderate | Water reminders, watch for symptoms |
| 91-103°F | High | Mandatory water breaks, buddy system |
| 104-115°F | Very High | Reduce work pace, frequent rest, consider rescheduling |
| > 115°F | Extreme | Stop outdoor work or use aggressive controls |

### Warning Signs
- **Heat Cramps**: Muscle pain, usually legs/abdomen. Move to shade, drink water, stretch.
- **Heat Exhaustion**: Heavy sweating, weakness, nausea, dizziness, headache. STOP WORK. Cool down immediately. If vomiting, call 911.
- **Heat Stroke**: Hot dry skin OR profuse sweating, confusion, loss of consciousness. CALL 911 IMMEDIATELY. Cool worker with water/ice while waiting.

### Discussion Questions
1. Where are the water coolers on site today?
2. What should you do if your coworker seems confused and stops sweating?
3. Who on this crew started in the last 2 weeks?

## Talk 5: Scaffold Safety

**Duration:** 7 minutes | **OSHA Standard:** 29 CFR 1926 Subpart L

### Key Points
- Scaffolds must support 4x their maximum intended load
- Competent person must supervise erection, moving, and dismantling
- Guardrails required at 10 feet (toprail 38-45 inches, midrail, toeboard)
- Planking: scaffold-grade lumber, extend 6-12 inches past support, no gaps > 1 inch
- No climbing on cross-braces — use built-in ladder or stair access

### Before Use Checklist
- [ ] Erected on solid, level base (mudsills on soft ground)
- [ ] All connections secure (pins, locks, braces)
- [ ] Guardrails, midrails, and toeboards in place
- [ ] Planking in good condition, properly overlapped
- [ ] Access ladder or stairs provided
- [ ] No overhead power lines within 10 feet
- [ ] Inspected by competent person today

## Talk 6: Crane & Rigging Safety

**Duration:** 8 minutes | **OSHA Standard:** 29 CFR 1926 Subpart CC

### Key Points
- Never stand under a suspended load
- Only certified operators may operate cranes
- Signal person required when operator cannot see load
- Maintain minimum clearance from power lines (10-50 ft depending on voltage)
- Load charts must be in the cab and followed — no exceptions

### Rigging Basics
- Inspect slings before each use (nylon: cuts, burns, melting; wire: broken wires, kinks, corrosion)
- Know your sling angles — load increases as angle decreases:
  - 90° angle: 100% of rated capacity
  - 60° angle: 87% of capacity
  - 45° angle: 71% of capacity
  - 30° angle: 50% of capacity
- Use softeners/padding on sharp edges
- Tag lines to control load rotation

## Talk 7: PPE — Proper Use and Inspection

**Duration:** 6 minutes | **OSHA Standard:** 29 CFR 1926 Subpart E

### Key Points
- PPE is the LAST line of defense, not the first
- Minimum on every construction site: hard hat, safety glasses, high-vis vest, steel-toe boots
- Additional PPE based on task: gloves, hearing protection, respirator, face shield, fall protection

### Inspection by Type
- **Hard Hat**: Check for cracks, dents, UV degradation (chalky feel). Replace every 5 years or after any impact.
- **Safety Glasses**: No cracks, scratches that impair vision, side shields intact
- **Gloves**: Match to hazard — cut-resistant for sharp materials, chemical-resistant for solvents, leather for welding
- **Hearing Protection**: Required when noise exceeds 85 dBA (you have to raise your voice at 3 feet)
- **Respirator**: Fit-tested, correct cartridge for the hazard, no facial hair that breaks seal

## Talk 8: Confined Space Entry

**Duration:** 8 minutes | **OSHA Standard:** 29 CFR 1926 Subpart AA

### Key Points
- A confined space: large enough to enter, limited entry/exit, not designed for continuous occupancy
- Permit-required if: hazardous atmosphere, engulfment potential, converging walls, or other serious hazard
- Atmospheric testing REQUIRED before entry: O2 (19.5-23.5%), LEL (<10%), CO (<25 ppm), H2S (<10 ppm)
- Attendant must remain at entry point at ALL times — never enter to rescue
- Rescue plan in place BEFORE entry begins

### Entry Procedure
1. Issue entry permit signed by entry supervisor
2. Isolate and lockout all energy sources
3. Test atmosphere (top, middle, bottom of space)
4. Ventilate continuously
5. Assign attendant
6. Entrant wears retrieval system (harness + retrieval line)
7. Maintain communication
8. Re-test atmosphere every 15-30 minutes

## Talk 9: Silica Exposure

**Duration:** 7 minutes | **OSHA Standard:** 29 CFR 1926.1153

### Key Points
- Crystalline silica is in concrete, brick, block, stone, morite, sand
- Cutting, grinding, drilling, or demolishing these materials creates respirable silica dust
- Silicosis is irreversible and can be fatal
- OSHA PEL: 50 micrograms per cubic meter (8-hour TWA)
- Table 1 compliance: follow the specified controls for each task

### Common Tasks & Required Controls (Table 1)
| Task | Required Controls |
|------|------------------|
| Concrete sawing (handheld) | Integrated water delivery + respirator |
| Concrete sawing (stationary) | Integrated water delivery |
| Concrete grinding (handheld) | Dust collection + respirator |
| Concrete drilling (handheld) | Dust collection or wet method |
| Jackhammering | Water spray + respirator |
| Tuck-pointing | Dust collection + respirator |

### Housekeeping
- Never dry sweep silica dust — use wet methods or HEPA vacuum
- Never use compressed air to clean silica dust from clothing

## Talk 10: Housekeeping & Fire Prevention

**Duration:** 6 minutes | **OSHA Standard:** 29 CFR 1926 Subpart F & Subpart D

### Key Points
- Good housekeeping prevents slips, trips, falls — the most common construction injury
- Clean as you go — don't let debris accumulate
- Keep walkways and stairs clear at all times
- Stack materials properly — no leaning, secure against tipping
- Fire extinguisher within 100 feet of flammable storage and within 50 feet of 5+ gallons of flammable liquid

### Daily Housekeeping Checklist
- [ ] Walkways clear of debris, cords, hoses
- [ ] Nails in scrap lumber bent over or removed
- [ ] Trash and packaging in dumpsters (not loose on floors)
- [ ] Materials stacked securely, not blocking exits
- [ ] Fire extinguishers accessible and charged
- [ ] Flammable materials properly stored (away from heat sources)
- [ ] Extension cords in good condition, not creating trip hazards
- [ ] Lighting adequate in work areas and stairways

### Hot Work
- Fire watch required for 30 minutes after welding/cutting
- 35-foot clearance from combustibles or use fire-resistant blankets
- Check below, behind, and adjacent areas for fire spread potential
